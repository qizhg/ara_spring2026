\noindent{\bf Abstract }
Spatial symmetry is one of the most effective inductive biases for learning in robotics, but in real multi-robot deployments it almost never holds exactly. We argue that the failures come in two structurally distinct forms — local breakers, visible in the current state but confined to small regions (e.g., fixed obstacles), and latent breakers, hidden from observation but fixed across an episode (e.g., wind or gravity acting on the whole team). The two are not interchangeable: state-dependent gating cannot find a breaker it does not observe, and hidden-context inference attributes all violations to a single global variable. We propose a dual-view theory and a unified multi-robot architecture that address both. View 1 formalizes a locally factored partially symmetric Markov game and shows that the critic-level symmetry error at a safe pair is controlled by its discounted future breaker exposure, motivating a TD-learned per-agent gated critic. View 2 formalizes a latent symmetry-breaker POMDP, bounds value error by breaker-estimation error, and establishes posterior equivariance — constraining the encoder design. The unified architecture pairs an equivariant latent encoder with per-agent state-dependent gating, and is evaluated on multi-robot tasks isolating each view and combining them.

\noindent{\bf Keywords}
cooperative multi-robot RL, group equivariance, symmetry breaking


\section{Introduction}
\paragraph{Symmetry in (multi-)robot learning.}
Group symmetries, geometric transformations that leave certain properties of a system invariant, provide one of the most effective inductive biases in robot learning.
This is because the physical world is rich in geometric structure: a navigation controller should not depend on the absolute orientation of the world frame, and a pick-and-place skill executed at one workspace location should transfer to a rotated or translated configuration of the same objects.
Encoding such structure directly into the learner reduces the effective hypothesis space and enables generalization from few samples, which has produced consistent gains in both imitation learning-based~\citep{} and reinforcement learning-based~\citep{} robot learning, where exploration is expensive and high-quality demonstrations are scarce.
To provide formality and prepare for theoretical grounding, we formalize symmetries in robot learning with the notion of group-symmetric Markov decision processes (MDPs)~\citep{}:
Given a group $G$ acting on the state space $\mathcal{S}$ and action space $\mathcal{A}$ of an MDP, the MDP is $G$-symmetric if the transition dynamics and reward function are invariant under the group actions, i.e., for every $g\in G$ and
$(s,a,s')\in\mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\times\mathcal{S}$,
\begin{align} \label{eq:G-symmetric MDP}
P(g \cdot s' | g \cdot s, g \cdot a) = P(s' | s, a), \quad R(g \cdot s, g \cdot a) = R(s, a)
\end{align}
where $g \cdot$ denotes the group action on the state and action spaces.

Although orginally developed for single-robot settings, the notion of group-symmetric MDPs can be extended to multi-robot settings by extending the group actions and invariance conditions in \eqref{eq:G-symmetric MDP} to the joint state, action, and observation spaces of all agents \cite{}.
In multi-robot coordination, the relevant group typically factors as $G = G_{\text{spatial}} \times \mathcal{S}_n$, where $G_{\text{spatial}}$ captures Euclidean symmetries (i.e., translations and rotations) and $\mathcal{S}_n$ captures permutation symmetry over $n$ homogeneous agents.
Commensurate with the time and resource constraints of this proposal, we will focus on the spatial symmetries in multi-robot coordination and leave permutation symmetry for future work. 

\paragraph{Multi-robot symmetry breakers: the local-latent dichotomy.}
In practice, however, the symmetry condition in \eqref{eq:G-symmetric MDP} almost never holds exactly and globally in real-world multi-robot tasks.
We observe that essentially every realistic violation falls into one of two structurally distinct modes, separated by a single diagnostic question:
is the symmetry-breaking signal present in an agent's current observation, or hidden from it? 
This {\em local-latent} dichotomy is the organizing principle of this proposal, and the two modes call for fundamentally different mechanisms:

{\em Local symmetry breaking} occurs when the symmetry-breaking signal is present in an agent's current observation.
This often happens in workspaces that contain fixed obstacles, walls, charging docks, asymmetric goal regions, etc. 
For example, for a team of warehouse robots navigating around a fixed shelving unit, 2D Euclidean symmetry is broken only in the immediate vicinity of the obstacle, whereas far from it, rotations and translations of the joint configuration leave the optimal team behavior invariant.
Crucially, in a typical configuration only a {\em subset} of the agents is actually near the obstacle at any given moment; the rest continue to operate in regions where rotational symmetry is exact. The breaker here is observable in the current
local state and localized to a small region of the per-agent state-action space.

{\em Latent symmetry breaking}, on the other hand, occurs when the symmetry-breaking signal is hidden from the agent's current observation and can potentially be inferred from historical information.
In this proposal, we focus on the common scenario where the symmetry-breaking signal is a latent global variable that is fixed within an episode but varies across episodes.
As examples, consider a team of aerial drones operating in a constant wind field, a multi-arm setup mounted on an inclined surface, and a fleet of underwater robots in a steady current, where all share the same structural property: the underlying dynamics would be {\em exactly symmetric if} the wind, gravity, or current vector were rotated together with the state and action, yey these vectors are not directly observable.
Such symmetry breakers are neither localized in the visible
state nor detectable from a single transition; they are latent variables that must be inferred from trajectory history.

The two modes are structurally orthogonal and not interchangeable. A method for local symmetry breaking has nothing to offer when the breaker is hidden, whereas a latent-inference method, conversely, attributes all symmetry violations to a single global variable and cannot efficiently represent localized violations whose effects depend on which agent is nearby. Realistic multi-robot deployments almost always exhibit both modes simultaneously, and we aim for a complete treatment that addresses both.

\paragraph{Departure from prior works.}
The focus of this proposal on the local-latent dichotomy separates it from prior works, which have either focused on single-agent settings where the dichotomy does not arise~\citep{}, or have taken a monolithic approach that globally relaxes the symmetry condition without distinguishing between structurally different modes of symmetry breaking~\citep{}.
Closing this gap is critical for the development of effective multi-robot learning algorithms and is the goal of this proposal.

\section{Methods}
We pursue two complementary views of spatial symmetry-breaking in multi-robot
systems. \textit{View~1} (\S\,2.2) develops the multi-agent theory of agent-wise local
breakers---when symmetry holds piecewise in each local factor---and yields a
gated critic that routes equivariant and raw branches based on a learned
state-dependent signal. \textit{View~2} (\S\,2.3) treats global hidden context (wind,
gravity, current) as a latent symmetry breaker and recovers equivariance in
the \textit{augmented} state through breaker inference. The two views are not
mutually exclusive: they correspond to different sources of symmetry violation
and can be combined within a single agent architecture (\S\,2.4).

\subsection*{Setting}

We consider a discounted cooperative Markov game
$\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathcal{N}, \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, r, \gamma\rangle$
with agent set $\mathcal{N} = \{1, \dots, N\}$ and joint action space
$\mathcal{A} = \mathcal{A}^1 \times \cdots \times \mathcal{A}^N$. Both views
assume a spatial group $G$ acting on the workspace, lifted to each agent's
state and action via representations
$(L_g^i, K_g^i)\colon \mathcal{X}^i \times \mathcal{A}^i \to \mathcal{X}^i \times \mathcal{A}^i$.
The two views differ in \textit{why} the symmetry $G$ may fail to be exact: View~1
attributes failures to localized regions in the observed local state--action
space; View~2 attributes failures to an unobserved global breaker variable.

\subsection*{View~1 --- Agent-wise local routing for partially symmetric Markov games}

\subsubsection*{Formal model}

We posit a \textit{locally factored partially symmetric Markov game} in which the
joint state, reward, and transition decompose agent-wise:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{S} = \mathcal{X}^1 \times \cdots \times \mathcal{X}^N, \qquad
r(s,a) = \sum_{i=1}^N r_i(x^i, a^i), \qquad
P(\cdot \mid s, a) = \bigotimes_{i=1}^N P_i(\cdot \mid x^i, a^i). \tag{2}
\end{equation}
Each local factor carries its own finite symmetry group $G_i$ acting on
$(\mathcal{X}^i, \mathcal{A}^i)$ and its own \textit{local breaker region}
$\mathcal{B}^i \subseteq \mathcal{X}^i \times \mathcal{A}^i$. On the safe set
$(x^i, a^i) \notin \mathcal{B}^i$ the local reward and transition are
$G_i$-invariant; on $\mathcal{B}^i$ they may be arbitrary. The breaker region
is itself $G_i$-invariant, so the safe/broken designation is a property of the
orbit, not of a representative element.

This formalism is more general than a single global gate over the joint state.
It allows symmetry to be exploited at agents whose local context is symmetric,
even when other agents' local contexts are not---recovering the multi-robot
nuance flagged in \S\,1.3(a).

\subsubsection*{Theoretical claims}

We pursue two theoretical results that, together, justify a gated critic
architecture.

\textbf{Claim T1 (one-step model exactness).} Define a routed model that, on each
factor, uses the group-averaged reward and transition on the safe set and the
raw local reward and transition on the breaker region:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\widetilde{r}_i(x^i, a^i) &:= (1-\lambda^i_{\text{cur}})\,\overline{r}_i(x^i, a^i) + \lambda^i_{\text{cur}}\,r_i(x^i, a^i),\\
\widetilde{P}_i &:= (1-\lambda^i_{\text{cur}})\,\overline{P}_i + \lambda^i_{\text{cur}}\,P_i,
\end{split}
\tag{3}
\end{equation}
with $\lambda^i_{\text{cur}}(x^i, a^i) := \mathbf{1}\{(x^i, a^i) \in \mathcal{B}^i\}$
and $\overline{r}_i, \overline{P}_i$ the $G_i$-orbit averages. We will show
that the routed Bellman operator $\widetilde{\mathcal{T}}$ coincides with the
true $\mathcal{T}$, so $\widetilde{Q}^\ast = Q^\ast$. This establishes that
oracle agent-wise local routing is \textit{exact at the one-step model level} and
isolates where the difficulty actually lies.

\textbf{Claim T2 (future breaker exposure controls critic symmetry error).}
One-step model symmetry does \textit{not} imply critic symmetry, because the critic
sums discounted future rewards along trajectories that may eventually enter
breaker regions. We will prove that for a safe-equivariant local policy
$\pi_i$ and any safe pair $(x, a) \notin \mathcal{B}^i$,
\begin{equation}
\bigl|\,Q^{\pi_i}_i(L^i_g[x], K^i_g[a]) - Q^{\pi_i}_i(x,a)\,\bigr|
\;\le\; 2\,V_{i,\max}\,\beta^{\pi_i}_i(x,a), \tag{4}
\end{equation}
where $\beta^{\pi_i}_i(x,a) := \mathbb{E}^{\pi_i}_{x,a}[\gamma^{\tau_i}]$ is
the \textit{discounted future breaker exposure} and $\tau_i$ is the first future
hitting time of $\mathcal{B}^i$. A finite-horizon variant replaces $\beta$
with $\eta^{\pi_i}_{i,H}(x,a) + \gamma^{H+1}$. The implication is that
symmetry bias on the critic is \textit{low-error} exactly where future breaker
exposure is small.

\subsubsection*{Algorithm: TD-learned gated critic}

Equation~(4) motivates gating at the \textit{critic level} rather than at the
representation level: the value-function statement is most directly
implemented by mixing two critic outputs. Concretely, we instantiate
\begin{itemize}
  \item an \textit{orbit-averaged invariant critic}
    $Q^{\text{inv}}_\theta(s, a) = \tfrac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G} f_\theta(g\!\cdot\!s, g\!\cdot\!a)$,
  \item an unconstrained \textit{raw critic} $Q^{\text{raw}}_\phi(s, a)$, and
  \item a learned gate
    $\widehat{\lambda}_\psi(s,a) = \sigma(g_\psi(s,a)) \in [0,1]$,
\end{itemize}
combined as
$Q^{\text{gate}}_{\theta,\phi,\psi}(s,a) =
(1-\widehat{\lambda}_\psi)\,Q^{\text{inv}}_\theta + \widehat{\lambda}_\psi\,Q^{\text{raw}}_\phi.$
A \textit{local-factor variant} sums per-agent gated branches and matches the
hypotheses of Claim~T2 more directly.

Critically, the gate is trained \textit{purely} through the TD loss---no auxiliary
supervision on breaker labels. The gradient
$\partial Q^{\text{gate}}/\partial \widehat{\lambda}_\psi
= Q^{\text{raw}}_\phi - Q^{\text{inv}}_\theta$
provides the routing signal: when the raw branch reduces TD error more than
the invariant branch at $(s,a)$, the gate opens; when the two branches agree,
the gate is free to favor the more data-efficient invariant branch. We
implement this within a CTDE actor--critic with decentralized actors
$a^i = \mu_i(o^i)$ and a centralized gated critic.

\subsubsection*{Baselines and ablations}

The empirical evaluation includes (i)~a raw MLP critic, (ii)~a fully invariant
critic, (iii)~a \textit{current-breaker oracle gate} using ground-truth
$\mathbf{1}\{(s,a) \in \mathcal{B}\}$---a diagnostic that tests whether
\textit{current} breaker information alone is sufficient---and (iv)~the TD-learned
gated critic (the main method). Per the future-exposure theory, we expect
(iii)~to be insufficient on tasks where trajectories cross breaker regions
several steps into the future, while (iv)~recovers the right gating signal
through TD bootstrapping.

\subsection*{View~2 --- Symmetry breakers as partially observed latent state}

\textbf{A note on notation.} Unlike View~1, the formalism in this subsection is
written in single-agent-style notation $(s, a, o, b)$ rather than the
agent-wise factorization $s = (x^1, \dots, x^N)$. This is deliberate: the
symmetry-breaking mechanism studied here is \textit{global} rather than agent-wise.
A hidden wind or gravity vector is a single latent variable shared by \textit{all}
agents in the team and fixed for the entire episode, so the relevant
structural object---augmented equivariance under simultaneous rotation of the
state, action, and breaker---does not decompose factor-by-factor in the way
View~1's local breaker regions do. Throughout \S\,2.3 we therefore use
$(s, a, o)$ to denote the joint state, joint action, and joint observation of
the team, with the understanding that each is composed of per-agent
quantities $s = (x^1, \dots, x^N)$, $a = (a^1, \dots, a^N)$,
$o = (o^1, \dots, o^N)$, and that the spatial group $G$ acts simultaneously on
all agents' local frames. The breaker $b$ remains a single global object. The
agent-wise factorization re-enters explicitly in \S\,2.4, where we combine
View~1 and View~2 in a properly multi-agent architecture.

\subsubsection*{Formal model}

For hidden-context breakers (\S\,1.3(b)), the agent-wise local-region view does
not fit: the breaker is fixed throughout the episode and never appears in the
observation. We instead model the environment as a \textit{latent symmetry-breaker
POMDP}
$\mathcal{M} = \langle \mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{O}, \mathcal{B}, P_b, r_b, \Omega_b, \gamma, \mu_0, \rho_0\rangle$
in which a hidden breaker $b \sim \rho_0$ is sampled at the start of each
episode and held fixed. The agent receives observations $o_t \sim \Omega_b(\cdot \mid s_t)$
and history $h_t = (o_0, a_0, r_0, \dots, o_t)$ but never sees $b$.

The structural assumption is \textit{augmented equivariance}: for every $g \in G$,
\begin{equation}
r_{g\cdot b}(g\!\cdot\!s, g\!\cdot\!a) = r_b(s,a), \quad
P_{g\cdot b}(g\!\cdot\!C \mid g\!\cdot\!s, g\!\cdot\!a) = P_b(C \mid s,a), \quad
\Omega_{g\cdot b}(g\!\cdot\!D \mid g\!\cdot\!s) = \Omega_b(D \mid s). \tag{5}
\end{equation}
The system is symmetric \textit{if and only if} $b$ is transformed alongside $(s,a)$.
Holding $b$ fixed while rotating $(s,a)$ therefore yields an incorrect
comparison, and the visible state alone admits no group action consistent with
the dynamics.

\subsubsection*{Theoretical claims}

\textbf{Claim T3 (no observation-only equivariance).} If the dynamics are
non-trivial in $b$, then any critic that enforces
$Q(g\!\cdot\!o, g\!\cdot\!a) = Q(o,a)$ on the observation alone is
inconsistent with the true Bellman targets at some $(o, a, g)$. The correct
invariance is $Q(g\!\cdot\!o, g\!\cdot\!a, g\!\cdot\!z) = Q(o, a, z)$
where $z$ is a breaker-sufficient statistic of history.

\textbf{Claim T4 (value sensitivity to breaker-estimation error).} Under
Lipschitz assumptions
$|r_b - r_{b'}| \le L_r d_\mathcal{B}(b, b')$ and
$\|P_b - P_{b'}\|_{\text{TV}} \le L_p d_\mathcal{B}(b, b')$,
conditioning on an estimate $\widehat{b}$ instead of the true $b$ yields a
fixed-point value error bounded by
$\tfrac{L_r + \gamma V_{\max} L_p}{1-\gamma}\,d_\mathcal{B}(b, \widehat{b}).$
This quantifies the benefit of better breaker inference and motivates
auxiliary objectives that drive $d_\mathcal{B}(b, \widehat{b})$ down.

\textbf{Claim T5 (equivariant belief update).} Under~(5) and an equivariant prior
$\rho_0$, the Bayesian posterior over $b$ transforms equivariantly:
$\beta^{g \cdot h_t}_t(g \cdot B) = \beta^{h_t}_t(B)$.
The encoder $f_\eta$ should therefore be built so that its output transforms
in the same representation as $b$ (e.g., a 2D vector for a 2D wind direction).

\subsubsection*{Algorithm: breaker-conditioned actor--critic}

The architecture has three components:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textit{Latent breaker encoder} $z_t = f_\eta(h_t)$, implemented as a recurrent
    network or a contrastive embedding. The encoder is designed so that
    $f_\eta(g \cdot h_t) = g \cdot f_\eta(h_t)$---i.e., its output lives in
    the same representation as $b$.

  \item \textit{Breaker-conditioned policy and critic}, equivariant in the augmented
    pair $(o, z)$:
    \[
      \pi_\theta(g\!\cdot\!a \mid g\!\cdot\!o, g\!\cdot\!z) = \pi_\theta(a \mid o, z),
      \qquad
      Q_\omega(g\!\cdot\!o, g\!\cdot\!a, g\!\cdot\!z) = Q_\omega(o,a,z).
    \]

  \item \textit{Auxiliary objectives} for the encoder, used in addition to the TD signal:
    a dynamics prediction loss
    $\mathcal{L}_{\text{dyn}} = \mathbb{E}[\ell(D_\psi(o_t, a_t, z_t), o_{t+1})]$,
    a contrastive loss that pulls together representations from the same
    episode (same $b$) and pushes apart those from different episodes, and an
    optional supervised loss
    $\mathcal{L}_{\text{sup}} = \mathbb{E}[d_\mathcal{B}(f_\eta(h_t), b)^2]$
    when simulator labels are available.
\end{enumerate}

We will explore three implementation variants: a \textit{point-estimate} encoder
producing $\widehat{b}_t \in \mathcal{B}$; a \textit{belief} representation
outputting distributional parameters; and \textit{canonicalization}, in which the
network rotates inputs into a frame aligned with $\widehat{b}_t$, applies a
standard network, and rotates outputs back---turning latent inference into a
learned coordinate gauge.

\subsubsection*{Baselines and ablations}

We compare against (i)~an MLP actor--critic with no symmetry prior; (ii)~a
strict observation-only equivariant model; (iii)~an \textit{oracle}
breaker-conditioned equivariant model that receives the true $b$, providing an
upper bound; (iv)~a recurrent non-equivariant model that has access to history
but no symmetry prior; and (v)~an \textit{observable-breaker ablation} that
exposes $b$ in the observation, confirming that the latent variable is indeed
the missing information.

\subsection*{Unifying perspective and combined architecture}

Although developed independently, the two views compose naturally because they
operate at \textit{different granularities}. View~1 introduces a \textit{per-agent,
state-dependent} mechanism: each local factor $(x^i, a^i)$ has its own breaker
region $\mathcal{B}^i$ and its own gate $\widehat{\lambda}^i$. View~2
introduces a \textit{global, per-episode} mechanism: a single latent breaker
$b \in \mathcal{B}$ is shared by the whole team and inferred once from the
joint trajectory history. The two mechanisms address orthogonal failure
modes---localized obstacle interactions on one hand, hidden physical context on
the other---and can be composed without conflict.

\textbf{Multi-agent setting.} As in \S\,2.2, the joint state and action factor
agent-wise: $s = (x^1, \dots, x^N)$ and $a = (a^1, \dots, a^N)$. As in \S\,2.3,
the team operates under partial observability with joint observation
$o_t = (o^1_t, \dots, o^N_t)$ and joint history
$h_t = (o_0, a_0, r_0, \dots, o_t)$. A single hidden breaker $b \sim \rho_0$
is sampled per episode and remains fixed; each agent $i$ also has its own
local symmetry group $G_i$ and local breaker region
$\mathcal{B}^i \subseteq \mathcal{X}^i \times \mathcal{A}^i$.

\textbf{Global breaker inference.} A shared encoder $z_t = f_\eta(h_t)$ ingests the
\textit{joint} history and produces a global breaker estimate that is broadcast to
every agent. The encoder is built to satisfy the augmented-equivariance
condition $f_\eta(g \cdot h_t) = g \cdot f_\eta(h_t)$---when the entire
team's history is rotated, the inferred latent rotates with it. In a CTDE
implementation, $f_\eta$ runs in the centralized critic and conditions all
per-agent branches.

\textbf{Per-agent gated, breaker-conditioned critic.} For each agent $i$, define a
\textit{breaker-conditioned invariant branch}
\begin{equation}
q^{\text{inv}}_{i,\theta}(x^i, a^i, z_t)
\;=\; \frac{1}{|G_i|}\sum_{g \in G_i} f_{i,\theta}\!\bigl(L^i_g[x^i],\,K^i_g[a^i],\,g \cdot z_t\bigr), \tag{6}
\end{equation}
which is equivariant in the \textit{augmented} per-agent triple $(x^i, a^i, z_t)$---
the breaker estimate transforms together with the local state and action,
following the augmented equivariance of View~2. Define a \textit{per-agent raw
branch} $q^{\text{raw}}_{i,\phi}(x^i, a^i, h_t)$ that has access to the joint
history and is otherwise unconstrained. Mix them with a per-agent gate
$\widehat{\lambda}^i_\psi(x^i, a^i) \in [0,1]$ from View~1:
\begin{align}
Q_i(x^i, a^i, h_t)
&= \bigl(1 - \widehat{\lambda}^i_\psi(x^i, a^i)\bigr)\,q^{\text{inv}}_{i,\theta}(x^i, a^i, z_t) \notag \\
&\quad +\; \widehat{\lambda}^i_\psi(x^i, a^i)\,q^{\text{raw}}_{i,\phi}(x^i, a^i, h_t). \tag{7}
\end{align}
The total centralized critic is the agent-wise sum
\begin{equation}
Q(s, a, h_t) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N Q_i(x^i, a^i, h_t), \tag{8}
\end{equation}
matching the factored value structure of~(2). This factorization is what
makes the local theoretical bound of Claim~T2 directly applicable to the
combined model: each summand individually satisfies the per-agent
future-exposure bound, conditioned on $z_t$.

\textbf{Reading the two mechanisms.} The invariant branch~(6) is the one that
carries the inductive bias from both views simultaneously. It exploits \textit{local}
$G_i$-equivariance in agent~$i$'s frame, but only insofar as the global hidden
context has been correctly inferred---captured by conditioning on $z_t$. The
per-agent gate $\widehat{\lambda}^i_\psi$ allows agent~$i$ to fall back to its
raw branch when its \textit{local} state--action pair lies in $\mathcal{B}^i$,
regardless of how well the global breaker has been estimated. Conversely, a
poor estimate $z_t$ does not corrupt agents whose gates are already
open---those agents already route through $q^{\text{raw}}_{i,\phi}$. The two
mechanisms therefore degrade gracefully and independently.

\textbf{Training.} The TD loss on the joint critic
\[
  L_Q = \mathbb{E}_\mathcal{D}\bigl[(Q(s_t, a_t, h_t) - y_t)^2\bigr]
\]
with $y_t = r_t + \gamma\,\overline{Q}(s_{t+1}, a'_{t+1}, h_{t+1})$
propagates gradients to all three parameter groups: the equivariant branches
$\{\theta\}$ and raw branches $\{\phi\}$, the per-agent gates $\{\psi\}$, and
the encoder $\eta$. The encoder additionally receives gradients from the
auxiliary objectives of \S\,2.3.3 (dynamics, contrastive, optional supervised).
No gate-supervision loss is used; the routing decisions emerge from TD
bootstrapping in the standard way.

\textbf{Empirical claim.} We will evaluate~(7)--(8) against the single-view
variants of \S\,2.2.3 and \S\,2.3.3, predicting that the combined architecture
matches the better of the two single-view models on each task family and
strictly dominates them on tasks that mix both failure modes (e.g., obstacles
\textit{and} hidden wind). Confirming this orthogonality is a primary empirical
claim of this proposal.

\subsection*{Experimental design}

We will evaluate across three task families, each chosen to isolate a
different combination of the symmetry-breaking modes:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Multi-robot navigation with fixed obstacles.} Cooperative
    goal-reaching on a 2D arena with a small number of fixed asymmetric
    obstacles. This isolates \textit{agent-wise local breakers} (View~1); a subset
    of agents at any given timestep is near an obstacle while others are not.
    Symmetry group $G = \mathrm{SO}(2)$ or a discrete subgroup.
    Symmetry-breaking strength is swept by varying obstacle density.

  \item \textbf{Hidden wind navigation.} Same as task~1 but with an
    obstacle-free arena and a per-episode constant wind vector omitted from
    observations. This isolates \textit{hidden global breakers} (View~2). The
    wind direction must be inferred from the discrepancy between commanded
    and realized displacement.

  \item \textbf{Combined regime.} A multi-arm manipulation task on an inclined
    surface (hidden gravity direction) with localized obstacles. This tests
    the combined architecture (\S\,2.4) and the orthogonality claim.
\end{enumerate}

Metrics follow standard practice---final return, sample efficiency, training
stability across seeds---supplemented by diagnostics specific to each view:
gate activation maps and their correlation with oracle breaker labels (View~1)
and angular error between $\widehat{b}_t$ and the true wind/gravity vector
(View~2). Performance will be reported as a function of symmetry-breaking
strength (obstacle density, wind magnitude) to characterize the regime where
each view, and the combined architecture, dominates.
